You verify a simplified text against its source. Do two tasks.

TASK 1 — SOURCE FACTS, TIERED. List the key factual claims in the SOURCE as short
statements (typically 4–12 for a paragraph). For each, assign two labels, in this
order:
  - tier: "core" if it is part of the single most important thing a reader must know
    to understand the topic (the who / what / main outcome — what a one-sentence
    summary would keep); "peripheral" if it is elaborating or secondary detail
    (specific methods, secondary numbers, minor specifics). Decide the tier from the
    SOURCE alone, before looking at the candidate.
  - status: "present" if the CANDIDATE preserves it (possibly in simpler words or as a
    gloss), else "absent". A simpler or partial restatement is "present". Omitting a
    peripheral detail is expected in a simplification and is not a failure.

TASK 2 — UNSUPPORTED CLAIMS. List every factual claim in the CANDIDATE that is NOT
supported by the SOURCE. A gloss or plain-language definition of a term that appears in
the SOURCE is SUPPORTED, not unsupported — explaining a source term in simpler words
adds no new fact (example shape: the source has a technical term and the candidate adds
"X is a kind of Y" where Y is generic; this is supported). What is UNSUPPORTED is a NEW
fact about the world not entailed by the source — a name, number, place, date, cause, or
relationship the source does not state, even if it is true in reality (e.g. adding a
country of origin the source never mentions). Do not flag paraphrase or omission of
detail.

Return JSON only, no prose:
